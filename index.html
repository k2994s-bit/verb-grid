The grid and the test

What each verb does to a user

Each row is a place in the app. Each column is how forceful the move is, green and gentle on the left, red and paternalistic on the right. Drag a verb to a new column and its journey changes, because strength is not neutral: too gentle and it is ignored, the middle is where behaviour shifts, too heavy and it is circumvented or it damages the connection it was never meant to touch. Select a verb to see this, and star the keepers for the shortlist below.

Strong — direct field experiment Moderate — adjacent or mixed evidence Thin — predicted from the principle

Start here, build your layer

Each starting point is a stack of verbs at set strengths. Stack as many as you like, they add to your layer, and where two overlap the most recent strength wins. Then open any verb to tune it. The verbs are the real controls.
Why does stacking ease the strong ones down?
One change usually helps. A pile of strong changes at once tends to backfire: people feel boxed in and either get anxious or switch the whole thing off. The evidence points the same way. Batching alerts a few times a day helped, but turning them off entirely made people more anxious (Fitz and colleagues, 2019). Hard blocks are the most worked-around tool there is (Lyngs and colleagues, 2019), and people under heavy limits take breaks and rebound (Haliburton and colleagues, 2024). The helpful zone is the middle. So as you stack more sets, the layer eases only the heaviest verbs back toward that middle, never pushes the gentle ones lower, and never goes below the middle. The exact step is a careful design choice, not a measured number, so you can override any verb by hand.
